<br><br>The date was February, 1874, the place was the exhibition-gallery of the St. Petersburg Architectural Association, and the show was devoted entirely to works by Victor Alexandrovich Hartmann, who had died very suddenly, at the age of 39, on July 23 of the previous year. Hartmann and Mussorgsky had been good friends, and at least one drawing in the Hartmann memorial exhibition had been lent by Mussorgsky himself.
The reason for the friendship of these two men was obvious enough. Both were members of that small group of artists in all fields who were then pursuing a new course, seeking inspiration in Russian history, Russian folk lore, and the everyday incidents of contemporary Russian life. This movement produced masterpieces in music and in literature, but in the visual arts it produced works of considerably less merit. In architecture, which was Hartmann's main province, it merely meant substituting one form of surface ornamentation for another, replacing Greco-Roman columns and pediments with carved stone fans, cocks, and patterns derived from peasant embroidery. That architecture is primarily the art of adapting space to use never seems to have entered Hartmann's head; it is therefore not surprising that most of his architectural projects, like the <i>Great Gate of Kiev</i>, were never realized in construction and that most of his actual achievement in architecture was intentionally ephemeral -- booths and buildings for fairs and that sort of thing.  Hartmann also did a good deal of craftwork, designed settings and costumes for ballet and opera, and drew countless sketches of everyday scenes for his own amusement. Each of these several types of expression is reflected by Mussorgsky, but especially the last.
