thetext=<HTML><BODY><b>The Bogatyr Gate (at Kiev, the Ancient Capital)</b>
According to all the contemporary accounts, the City Council of Kiev announced a competition for a ceremonial gateway to commemorate the event of April 4, 1866, but we are never told what happened on April 4, 1866. What the censors are hiding is the fact that on that date the Nihilists of Kiev threw a bomb at the Czar, who was not injured by it. The gateway was to commemorate the Czar's escape.
For lack of funds, the competition was called off almost as soon as it had been announced, and no Kievan gate was ever erected. <a href="asfunction:showImage,9"><font color="#FF0000"><u>Hartmann's design</u></font></a> for it involved an arch resting upon two columns whose capitals are bigger than their shafts; the intended effect was that the columns had sunk deep into the ground with age. On the arch itself was an inscription in Old Slavonic: "Blessed he that comes in the name of the Lord." Above this was a chapel with a stained glass window surmounted by a kind of hood of pierced, carved stonework with a figure of the Archangel Michael on a shield and the Russian imperial eagle at the very top. To the right of the arch was a belfry in the form of an old Slavonic war-helmet. The belfry, the roof and the two smaller arches on either side of the main one were to have been covered with brilliant, vari-colored tile.
Hartmann's design for a ceremonial gate inspired Mussorgsky to a movement suggesting a ceremonial procession, with the clanging of bells, the chanting of priests, and the Promenade theme, which is here employed for three reasons--because it is itself a processional tune, because it identifies Mussorgsky himself with the use of old Slavonic motifs in the design of the gate and so places his stamp of approval on them, and because it ties the whole suite together with the final statement of a theme which has often been heard on previous pages.
