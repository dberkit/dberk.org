<i>Bydlo</i>. A Polish word meaning cattle. On his way back from Western Europe in 1869, Hartmann spent a month in the Polish town of Sandomir (the locale of the third act of Mussorgsky's <i>Boris Godounoff</i>) and there he drew and painted many sketches of ghetto types and happenings, including the <i>Two Polish Jews</i> which Mussorgsky had owned. <i>Bydlo</i> is based upon a sketch of an ox-cart with huge wooden wheels lumbering along a muddy road. It has become traditional to start this movement <i>pianissimo</i> and increase the volume of the music as it progresses, after the fashion of the once-celebrated band piece called <i>The Turkish Patrol</i>, but Mussorgsky himself opened <i>Bydlo fortissimo</i> and kept to that dynamic level almost until the end. (<a href="asfunction:showImage,3"><font color="#FF0000"><u>This image</u></font></a> of a Polish market woman is one of Hartmann's few surviving sketches of the Sandomir ghetto.)
