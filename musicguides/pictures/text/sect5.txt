thetext=<HTML><BODY><b>Promenade</b>
This movement is intended to describe the composer himself, strolling about from picture to picture in the gallery. It is heard six times in all (the last time under the title <i>Con Mortuis in Lingua Mortua</i>) and its theme recurs in <i>The Great Gate of Kiev</i>.  It has a different color and character each time it appears, but its alterations of 5/4 and 6/4 are invariably a little heavy, awkward, and waddly; Mussorgsky weighed well over 200 pounds. The changes in the character of the movement are, of course, intended to convey the different changes of feeling which Mussorgsky took with him from picture to picture.  The <i>Promenade</i> is vigorous and confident at the beginning, quietly expectant as the composer turns to <i>Il Vecchio Castello</i>, suddenly shocked with surprise as his eye lights on the <i>Ballet of the Chicks in Their Shells</i>, and full of grandiose, chest-thumping pride as he strides delightedly away from the <i>Two Polish Jews</i>. The reason for this last inflection of the <i>Promenade</i> is apparent when one studies the catalogue of the Hartmann Memorial Exhibition: <i>Two Polish Jews</i> was Mussorgsky's own property, and his ownership of this sketch is acknowledged in that document.
