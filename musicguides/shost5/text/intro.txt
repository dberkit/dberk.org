thetext=Ever since its premiere, Shostakovich's Fifth Symphony has been one of the composer's most popular and controversial works.  At the first performance, audience members reportedly wept openly during the third movement, and responded after the finale with an ovation that lasted longer than the work itself.  The meaning of the symphony, though, was immediately contested ground.  Some heard the piece as a challenge to the Soviet authorities, while others construed as an exemplary instance of socialist realism in music.  In official publications, Shostakovich described the symphony as an autobiographical work, about "the suffering of man, and all-conquering optimism," even quoting with approval a critic's suggestion that the symphony was his answer as a Soviet artist to criticism of his earlier works.  More recent commentators, many influenced by Solomon Volkov's Testimony, have found in the piece specific musical criticism of Stalin's regime.  Whatever the intended (or perceived) meaning of the piece, though, it is extraordinarily effective as a purely musical construct.  This listening guide will not address questions of interpretation, but will provide some suggestions about the structure of its first movement.
